## The Attendance-Revenue Trap

Every pastor knows the pattern. Holiday Sunday, rainy Sunday, long weekend — attendance drops, and so does the offering. It happens like clockwork: fewer people in the seats means less money in the basket.

Here is the math. If your church has 500 regular attendees giving an average of NGN 2,000 per Sunday, a full Sunday brings in NGN 1,000,000. When 30% of your congregation misses a single Sunday, you lose NGN 300,000 that week.

And it is not just public holidays. Think about:
- **Travel season** (December/January) — families travel, attendance thins out for weeks
- **Rainy season Sundays** — heavy downpours keep members at home
- **Public holidays** — Independence Day, Democracy Day, Eid periods
- **Traffic and heat** — a hot Saturday night keeping people home Sunday morning
- **Personal travel** — work trips, family visits, weddings out of town

Count the reduced Sundays honestly: most churches experience **at least 10-15 Sundays per year** where attendance drops by 20-40%.

> **What it's costing your church:**
>
> [Average Sunday offering] x [% attendance drop] x [Number of reduced Sundays per year]
>
> For a church averaging NGN 1M per Sunday with 12 reduced Sundays at 30% drop:
> **That is NGN 3.6 million per year that simply does not arrive.**

## Why "Just Transfer It" Doesn't Work

Most churches have tried the obvious solution: announce from the pulpit, "If you cannot attend next Sunday, please transfer your offering."

Why it consistently fails:

1. **People forget.** Life happens. The intention is real on Sunday morning, but by Monday it has faded. By Tuesday, it is forgotten entirely.

2. **No dedicated church account publicized.** Members do not have the bank details handy when the moment of giving comes. They would have to search for it, ask someone, or wait — and waiting usually means it never happens.

3. **No categorization.** A bank transfer is just a lump sum. The church cannot tell tithes from offerings from building fund contributions. Your finance team has to guess or follow up manually.

4. **No receipt.** Members have no record of their giving for personal accountability, tax purposes, or simply the peace of mind that their contribution was received and recorded.

5. **No follow-up mechanism.** The church has no way to know who intended to give but did not. There is no system to gently remind or track consistency.

**The underlying issue:** You are asking people to do something manually, repeatedly, with no system to support them. That is a system design problem, not a faithfulness problem.

It is not that your members do not want to give. It is that there is no system making it easy for them to give consistently.

## The 3 Types of Church Income

Understanding your income mix is the first step to financial stability.

### Type 1: Spontaneous Giving
One-time offerings, visitor gifts, special appeals. This income is unpredictable by nature — it depends entirely on who is present and how they feel in the moment.

### Type 2: Seasonal Giving
Harvest thanksgiving, Christmas, Easter, special projects. The timing is predictable, but the amounts vary widely year to year.

### Type 3: Committed Recurring Giving
The backbone of financially healthy churches. Same amount, same frequency, rain or shine, travel or not. This is the income you can plan around — the income that lets you budget for staff salaries, building maintenance, outreach programs, and growth.

**The healthiest churches worldwide have 50-70% of their income from Type 3.** Most Nigerian churches are 80% or more dependent on Type 1 — entirely reliant on who physically shows up on Sunday.

> The goal is not to eliminate spontaneous giving. It is to build a **predictable floor of recurring income** so your church can plan, budget, and grow with confidence.

## The Free Fix — Do This Today

You do not need to buy any software to start solving this problem. Here are five steps you can implement this week:

### Step 1: Open a Dedicated Offering Account
If you do not already have one, open a separate church offering account (distinct from your operations account). This makes giving by transfer clean and trackable.

### Step 2: Print the Account Details on a Card
Create a simple card with your bank name, account number, and church name. Hand one to every member. Make it wallet-sized so they keep it with them.

### Step 3: Announce It Every Sunday
Every single Sunday, verbally remind the congregation: "If you are travelling next week or cannot attend for any reason, you can still give — here are the bank details." Repetition is key. Say it until people can recite it.

### Step 4: Have Personal Conversations with Key Givers
For your most committed givers (your top 20-30 members), have a personal conversation: "Would you be willing to set up a standing order with your bank for your monthly tithe?" Standing orders are free at most Nigerian banks.

### Step 5: Create a Simple Tracking Sheet
Set up a basic spreadsheet: Member Name | Pledged Amount | Frequency | Jan | Feb | Mar | and so on. Track who is consistent and who might need a gentle follow-up.

> **An honest note:** This approach works — but it depends on members remembering, banks processing standing orders reliably (they often do not), and manual tracking by your finance team. It is a good start, but it breaks down at scale.
