# Price Handling Guide
## How to Handle Pricing Conversations with Nigerian Clients

*Based on: Psychology of Selling (Brian Tracy), Sell or Be Sold (Grant Cardone), How to Sell to Nigerians (Akin Alabi), $100M Offers (Alex Hormozi), Mike's April 6 internal meeting directive*

---

## The Rules

### Rule 1: Never Drop the Price
**Cardone:** "Do NOT drop the price. Do NOT move to a cheaper option. Move UP to a more expensive option. Build MORE value, not less price."

**Mike (April 6):** "We shouldn't drop. We should make our price fair. We shouldn't drop it. If there's an objection, we take out a feature. That's all."

When a client pushes back on price, you have two moves:
- **Move UP** — show them a bigger package where the per-unit value is better
- **Remove a feature** — "We can take out [feature] which brings it to [lower amount]. But that's the part that [solves their hot button]. Is that what you want to remove?"

Never give a discount. Never negotiate the number down. The moment you drop, you've told them your original price was inflated.

---

### Rule 2: Price Is Never the Real Issue
**Cardone:** "Price objection = buyer doesn't love it enough yet. People buy what they LOVE regardless of price. No one spends NGN 10M on something worth NGN 10M. They spend NGN 10M when they believe they're getting value in EXCESS."

**Tracy:** "Suitability > Quality > Price. The price objection is almost always a disguise for something else — either they don't see enough value, or they're afraid it won't work."

When someone says "it's too expensive" — they're really saying "I'm not convinced the value exceeds the cost." Your job is to build more value, not reduce cost.

---

### Rule 3: People Buy Emotionally, Justify Logically
**Tracy:** "People are 100% emotional. They decide emotionally and justify logically."

**Mike (April 6):** "When we are talking to this client, we want to put them in a position where they are emotionally sold on what we're trying to sell to them."

This means:
- The emotional sell happens FIRST — they need to FEEL the pain of their current situation and FEEL the relief of the solution
- The logical justification (numbers, ROI, timeline) comes SECOND — this gives them permission to say yes
- If you lead with price before they're emotionally invested, you lose

---

### Rule 4: Nigerians Want to Feel Like They're Winning
**Alabi:** "Nigerians would rather pay MORE when they feel they are ripping you off than pay less when they feel you are ripping them off."

This means:
- Stack the value so high that the price feels like a fraction of what they're getting
- Never show individual line-item costs — show "Included" next to every item with a bundled total
- When they see 15 items included for one price, the price feels small
- When they see 3 items, they calculate per-item and feel ripped off
- The Nigerian buyer who feels he's getting NGN 20M of value for NGN 10M will pay happily

---

### Rule 5: Anchor Against the Cost of NOT Buying
**Tracy:** "Fear of loss is 2.5x stronger than desire for gain."

Before you EVER state your price, state the cost of their current problem:

> "You're losing NGN 1.08M/year to Airbnb commissions."
> "You're spending NGN 250,000/month on manual reconciliation."
> "You've had 3 disputed balances this year at NGN 500,000 each."
> "Giving drops 40% during 8 holiday weeks — that's NGN 1.6M/year lost."

THEN state your price. It feels tiny against the loss they're already absorbing.

The client isn't choosing between "buy now" and "save money." They're choosing between "stop the bleeding now" and "keep bleeding for 3 more months while you think about it."

---

### Rule 6: The Second Money Is Easier Than the First
**Cardone:** "Once someone says yes to the first purchase, the second yes (accessories, upgrades, add-ons) is dramatically easier. ALWAYS upsell after the close."

After they agree to the main deal:
> "And because we're doing the full deployment, I'll include the staff training and user manual at no extra charge."

They feel like they're getting more. You haven't changed the price — you've added perceived value.

---

### Rule 7: State the Guarantee BEFORE the Price
**Hormozi:** Remove perceived risk to increase perceived value.
**Alabi:** "Nigerians have been burned. They expect to be manipulated."

Before you say the number, say the guarantee:
> "If the system isn't working within 30 days, we keep working until it does — at no additional cost. We don't walk away from a deployment."

THEN say the price. The guarantee lowers the fear, which makes the price feel safer.

## The Objection Playbook

### "The price is too high"

| Step | What You Do | Why |
|------|------------|-----|
| 1. Agree | "You're right — it's a significant investment." | Cardone: never argue. Agreement lowers resistance. |
| 2. Redirect to their pain | "You mentioned you're losing NGN [X] per month from [problem they told you]." | Tracy: use THEIR words and THEIR numbers. |
| 3. Build MORE value | Show additional benefits they haven't considered. Stack the value higher. | Alabi: they need to feel like they're getting a steal. |
| 4. Move UP, not down | "Actually, let me show you the option that includes [bigger scope] — the value per unit is better." | Cardone: moving up reframes from "how do I spend less" to "how do I get more." |
| 5. If still resisting, remove a feature | "We can take out [feature] which brings it to [amount]. But that's the part that [solves hot button]. Is that what you want to remove?" | Mike's directive. Never discount — remove scope. |

**Example dialogue:**

**Client:** "Ten million is too much. We were thinking three to four."

**You:** "I hear you. Ten million is a significant investment — you're absolutely right about that."

*(Pause. Don't rush.)*

**You:** "You mentioned you're losing about NGN 400,000 a month from no-shows and double bookings. That's NGN 4.8 million a year walking out the door."

**You:** "This system eliminates that entirely. Guests pay upfront before they get an access code. No payment, no code, no entry. That alone recovers your investment in about two years — and that's before we count the time your staff saves and the Airbnb commissions you stop paying."

**You:** "Would you like to move forward?"

---

### "Let me think about it"

**Tracy:** "'Think it over' = goodbye forever. They forget you the minute you walk out."

**You:** "You said at the start you'd tell me honestly if this makes sense. From everything you've shared, this seems like exactly what you need — unless there's something I'm not understanding?"

*(Silence. Do NOT speak. The buying takes place in the silence.)*

If they name a concern — NOW you know the real objection (it was never "let me think about it").

---

### "We can get it cheaper elsewhere"

**Cardone:** "I don't come with that transaction."

**You:** "You should absolutely explore your options. If their solution genuinely does what you need, go with them. Seriously."

*(This builds massive trust — they expect you to fight.)*

**You:** "What I can tell you is that I come with this transaction. You have my number, our office address. If something breaks at 9pm on a Friday, you're calling me — not a helpdesk in another city."

---

### "We don't have the budget right now"

**Cardone:** "There is NO shortage of money on this planet."

**You:** "I understand. Let me ask — if budget wasn't a factor, would you want this system?"

If yes: "Then let's talk about how to make it work. What if we started with [core module] first? That addresses your biggest pain point immediately. We phase in the rest once you're seeing value."

If no: it was never about budget — dig deeper.

---

### "Can you give us a discount?"

**You:** "I appreciate you asking. Our pricing reflects the full value of what we deliver — the development, the deployment, the training, and the life-long support. We don't discount because we don't inflate."

**You:** "What I can do is adjust the scope. If you want to bring the investment down, we can remove [feature X]. That brings it to [amount]. But I want to be transparent — [feature X] is what handles [their hot button]. Would you want to remove that?"

*(They almost always say no — which means they've just agreed the full price is justified.)*

---

### "That's double my budget"

**Cardone's script:**

**You:** "I'm aware this is double your budget. Allow me to share why I'm showing you this option despite that."

Then present the value that justifies the higher price. If the value is clear, the budget expands. Budgets are not fixed — they're a reflection of perceived value.

## The Presentation Order (When to Talk Price)

**Tracy:** "Price out of place kills the sale."

| Order | What You Do | Why |
|-------|------------|-----|
| 1 | Diagnose — understand their pain | You need their numbers for the anchor |
| 2 | Position — present the solution using THEIR words | They need to be emotionally invested first |
| 3 | Stack the value — list everything included | The stack must feel overwhelming BEFORE the price |
| 4 | State the guarantee | Remove fear BEFORE the number |
| 5 | Anchor against cost of inaction | "You're losing NGN X/month" — the loss must feel bigger than the price |
| 6 | THEN state the price | Now it feels small against the value and the loss |
| 7 | Ask for the order | "Based on everything we've discussed, can we move forward?" |

If you state price at step 2 instead of step 6 — the sale dies. They haven't felt enough value yet.

## How to Present the Price

### DO:
- Bundle everything into one total — "The total investment is NGN [X]"
- List every item as "Included" — make the stack feel massive
- Use the word "investment" not "cost" or "price"
- Present in the meeting, not by email
- State the guarantee first, price second
- Have the math ready: "This costs NGN [X]. You're currently losing NGN [Y]/year. The system pays for itself in [Z] months."

### DON'T:
- Show individual line-item costs (they'll negotiate each line)
- Use the words "cost", "charge", "fee", or "expense"
- Email the price without presenting it in person first
- State the price before they're emotionally invested
- Apologise for the price or qualify it ("I know it's a lot, but...")
- Round down ("about 9 million" when it's 10 million — just say 10)

## The Nigerian Price Psychology (Alabi)

Nigerian buyers have specific behaviours around pricing:

1. **They will always negotiate.** This is cultural. Expect it. Don't be offended. But don't cave.

2. **They test whether the price is real.** The first pushback is a test. If you drop immediately, they know the price was fake. If you hold firm and explain the value, they respect it.

3. **They value what costs more.** A system priced at NGN 3M feels inferior to one priced at NGN 10M — even if they do the same thing. Low price signals low quality in Nigeria.

4. **They buy from people they trust.** Trust is built by: showing up prepared, having physical materials, keeping promises, responding fast, and being patient with their questions. The seller who exercises "superhuman patience" (Alabi) wins.

5. **They tell other people what they paid.** If you discount, they tell other prospects "I got it for 7 million, just push them." If you hold firm, they tell others "it's 10 million and it's worth it." Your pricing integrity protects future deals.

6. **Service justifies premium.** Alabi: "If you treat a customer right like a king or a queen, you already won their heart and their money." In Nigeria, most businesses give terrible service. If you respond fast, follow up, deliver on time — you can charge more because the service IS the differentiator.

✗ Apologise for the price
✗ Show line-item costs
✗ State price before building value
✗ Email the price without presenting it

✓ Agree first
✓ Use their words and numbers
✓ State guarantee before price
✓ Present in person
✓ Ask for the order (at least 3 times)
