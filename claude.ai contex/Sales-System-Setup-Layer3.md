## The 4-Step Sales Process

### Step 1: DIAGNOSE

**What it is:** Research + discovery. Understand the business, the pain, and the person BEFORE you pitch anything.

**Before the meeting:**
- Research the company (Google, LinkedIn, website, CAC if available)
- Research the contact person (LinkedIn, social media)
- Review any notes from the setter (Faridah) — what did they say on the initial call?
- Identify which Algolog product/SDS solution likely fits
- Prepare 2-3 industry-specific pain points to reference

**In the meeting — discovery questions:**

| Question | Why You Ask It |
|----------|---------------|
| "Tell me about your business — what do you do day to day?" | Let them talk. Hot button often appears in the first 2 minutes. |
| "What's the biggest challenge you're dealing with right now?" | Gets the pain in their own words. Use their words back to them later. |
| "How are you currently handling [the process they described]?" | Reveals the manual work, the gaps, the frustration. |
| "On a scale of 1-10, how would you rate your current setup?" | Nobody says 10. Whatever they say → "What would make it a 10?" |
| "What would make it a 10?" | They tell you exactly what to sell them. This IS the hot button. |
| "How long have you been dealing with this?" | Establishes urgency — the longer, the more it costs. |
| "What happens if nothing changes in the next 6-12 months?" | Fear of loss (2.5x stronger than desire for gain — Tracy). |
| "Who else is involved in this decision?" | Identify ALL decision-makers now, not after the meeting. |

**Rules:**
- Do NOT pitch during diagnosis. Listen.
- Take notes. Write down their exact words — you'll use them in Step 2.
- Ask follow-up questions: "How do you mean?" (Tracy's universal question — works on ANY statement)
- Keep the spotlight on THEM (Tracy: spotlight model — whoever is talking has the light on them. Keep it on the customer.)
- Attention span is ~3 sentences before they drift. Ask questions every 3 sentences to recapture 100%.

### Step 2: POSITION

**What it is:** Present the solution using THEIR words, THEIR pain, THEIR numbers. Not a feature dump.

**Framework: "Because of this... you can... which means..."**

| Element | Example (Spacer for short-let) |
|---------|-------------------------------|
| **Product Feature** | "Because Spacer generates unique access codes tied to each booking..." |
| **Product Benefit** | "...you can eliminate physical key handovers entirely..." |
| **Customer Benefit** | "...which means you don't need someone at the desk 24/7, and guests can arrive at any time." |

**Rules:**

1. **Lead with the hot button.** You identified it in Step 1. Don't present 10 features equally — present the ONE thing they care about most, then move to close. (Tracy: Ascending Close — when you find the hot button, STOP presenting and close.)

2. **Use their words.** "You mentioned that you're spending 3 hours every Sunday counting cash..." — this proves you listened and makes the solution feel personal.

3. **Show, don't tell.**
   - Tracy: 22x more nerves eye-to-brain than ear-to-brain
   - Cardone: "People believe what they SEE, not what they hear"
   - Always have a live demo ready (Spacer booking portal on your phone, dashboard screenshots, real client data)
   - Always show proof in writing — proposal, one-pager, printed materials

4. **Don't overwhelm.** One key feature is enough to move to close (Mike's directive from April 6 meeting). If they're nodding at feature #3, don't keep going to feature #10.

5. **Trial close throughout.** After each benefit:
   - "Does this make sense so far?"
   - "Would this be an improvement on what you have now?"
   - "Is this what you had in mind?"
   - A "no" to a trial close is feedback on ONE feature, not total rejection.

**Positioning by buyer type (Tracy — 6 types):**

| Type (25% each) | How to Spot Them | How to Present |
|-----------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Analytical** | Detail-focused, slow, asks lots of questions. Accounting, engineering, IT. | Prove it on paper. Data, numbers, comparisons. Don't rush. |
| **Relator** | People-oriented, checks with everyone, wants to be liked. HR, teaching, nursing. | Build the relationship first. Show who else uses it. Testimonials matter most. |
| **Driver** | Direct, impatient, bottom-line. Entrepreneurs, execs. | Speed up. Skip small talk. Lead with ROI. "Here's what it does, here's what it costs." |
| **Socializer** | Outgoing, talks about achievements, may agree too quickly. | Let them talk. Write everything down (they may forget what they agreed to). Get commitment in writing. |

### Step 3: HANDLE OBJECTIONS

**Mindset:**
- Objections are NOT rejection. They're buying signals — the person is engaged enough to push back. (Cardone: "Emotional outbursts = buyer is CLOSE to buying")
- Never argue. Never make them wrong. Never disagree. (Cardone: "The moment you argue, you've lost — even if you're right")
- Always agree FIRST, then redirect.

**Universal response:** "How do you mean?" (Tracy) — works on ANY objection. Buys you time, makes them clarify, and often they talk themselves out of their own objection.

**The Objection Playbook:**

#### "The price is too high"

| Step | What to Do |
|------|-----------|
| 1. Agree | "You're right — it's a significant investment." |
| 2. Redirect to their pain | "You mentioned you're losing NGN X per month from [problem they told you]." |
| 3. Build MORE value | Show additional benefits they haven't considered. |
| 4. Move UP, not down | "Actually, let me show you the option that includes [bigger scope] — the value per unit is better." |
| 5. NEVER drop the price | If needed, remove a feature: "We can take out [feature] which brings it to [amount]. But that's the part that [solves hot button]. Is that what you want to remove?" |

**Why never drop:** Cardone — "When people are buying, they don't buy out of logical analysis. They buy from an emotional standpoint." If they love it, they'll find the money. If they don't love it, no discount fixes that. Dropping price also trains the buyer that your prices are negotiable and inflated.

#### "Let me think about it"

| Step | What to Do |
|------|-----------|
| 1. Approach close callback | "You said at the start you'd tell me honestly if this makes sense. From everything you've shared, it seems like exactly what you need — unless there's something I'm not understanding?" |
| 2. Silence | Do NOT speak. Let them fill the gap. (Tracy: "The buying takes place in the silence") |
| 3. Dig for real objection | "Make sure of what exactly?" — they'll reveal the real concern (usually money or fear). |
| 4. Address the real concern | Now you know what to solve. |

**Remember:** "Think it over" = goodbye forever. They forget you the moment you leave. 48% of salespeople never ask for the close even once.

#### "I need to check with my partner/board"

| Step | What to Do |
|------|-----------|
| 1. Agree | "That makes complete sense." |
| 2. Get in the room | "Rather than you having to present our solution, what if I joined that meeting? Even 15 minutes." |
| 3. If refused, go direct | "Would it help if I sent the decision-maker a brief message introducing what we discussed?" |
| 4. Get the contact | "Do you have their number? I'll send a short WhatsApp." |

**Never** let your champion sell for you. They will dilute, forget, or misrepresent the value.

#### "We already have a system"

| Step | What to Do |
|------|-----------|
| 1. Agree | "That's great — it sounds like you've got things running." |
| 2. The 1-10 question | "On a scale of 1-10, how would you rate it?" |
| 3. Find the gap | "What would make it a 10?" |
| 4. Fill the gap | Position your solution as completing what they have, not replacing it. |

#### "We can get this cheaper elsewhere"

| Step | What to Do |
|------|-----------|
| 1. Agree | "You should absolutely explore your options." |
| 2. Build trust | "If their solution genuinely does what you need, go with them. Seriously." |
| 3. Differentiate on service | "What I can tell you is that I come with this transaction. You have my number, our office address. If something breaks at 9pm Friday, you're calling me — not a helpdesk." |
| 4. Close | "Would it make sense to proceed with us, knowing that?" |

#### "Send me a proposal"

| Step | What to Do |
|------|-----------|
| 1. Don't refuse | "I'd love to put something together." |
| 2. Trade for meeting | "To make sure it actually addresses what matters to your business, I'd need 15 minutes to understand your setup. Would Thursday or Friday work?" |
| 3. Safety phrase | "If after 15 minutes you don't see value, I'll say thank you and that's it." |

**Never** send a generic proposal. A proposal without discovery is easy to ignore.

#### "We're not ready / too small for this"

| Step | What to Do |
|------|-----------|
| 1. Don't accept the belief | Share a smaller client story: "One of our clients in Kaduna has 12 units — smaller than you. She was managing everything on WhatsApp..." |
| 2. Reframe | "The question isn't whether you're ready for technology — it's whether you're ready to stop doing manually what a system can do for you automatically." |
| 3. Low-risk next step | "Let me set up a demo with YOUR rooms, YOUR pricing. You can see exactly how it looks before committing." |

#### "Not the right time"

| Step | What to Do |
|------|-----------|
| 1. Agree | "I understand. Timing matters." |
| 2. Future trigger question | "If ever you were to move forward with something like this, what would cause you to?" (Tracy) |
| 3. Silence | They reveal the real objection. |
| 4. Follow up | Cardone: follow up for years. Varied types — call, WhatsApp, email, visit. Never bring up unreturned calls. |

### Step 4: CLOSE

**Mindset:**
- Closing is a SEPARATE skill from selling. Train it separately. (Cardone)
- Most buyers only buy after you've asked 5 times. Most salespeople quit after 1. (Tracy)
- A planned presentation is 20x more powerful than a spontaneous one. (Tracy)
- ALWAYS present figures. 63% of companies never even make a proposal — just proposing is a competitive advantage. (Cardone: mystery shop of 500+ companies)

**Closing techniques:**

#### The Approach Close (set up at START of meeting)
> "Thank you for your time. All I ask is that you look at what I have to show you with an open mind, and at the end, tell me honestly whether or not it makes sense. Is that fair?"

If they later say "let me think about it":
> "You promised you'd tell me one way or another. After everything you've told me, this seems ideal — unless there's something I don't understand?"

#### The Demonstration Close
> "If I could show you [the best solution for your situation], are you in a position to make a decision today?"

Changes the conversation from "will you listen" to "can you invest if I deliver?" They can't say "can't afford it" later — they already confirmed they could.

#### The Assumptive Close
Talk as if they already own it:
> "When we deploy this for you, the first thing your team will notice is..."
> "You're really going to appreciate how the dashboard gives you visibility into..."

#### The Hot Button Close
1. Identify the ONE thing they want most (from diagnosis)
2. Focus ALL energy on that one benefit
3. Repeat it throughout the presentation
4. When closing: "You said [hot button] was the most important thing. This delivers exactly that. Shall we get started?"

#### The Phased Start Close (for budget resistance)
> "What if we started with [core module] first? That addresses your biggest pain point immediately. We can phase in the rest once you're seeing the value."

Not a discount. Same total. Lower entry point.

#### The 90-Day Checkpoint Close (for risk resistance)
> "Let's deploy and build in a 90-day review. At 90 days, we look at the numbers together. If it's not delivering, we figure out what needs to change."

#### The Direct Ask
> "Based on everything we've discussed, I think this is exactly what you need. Can we move forward?"

Then **silence**. Do not speak until they respond.

## Post-Meeting Protocol

After EVERY meeting — won or lost — answer these:

| Question | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| What was the hot button? | Did I identify it? Did I press it enough? |
| How many times did I ask for the close? | If less than 3, I didn't try hard enough. |
| What objections came up? | Add to the objection diary with responses for next time. |
| Did I talk too much or listen enough? | Spotlight should be on THEM 70%+ of the time. |
| What would I do differently? | One specific thing to improve next time. |
| Did I get a next step? | If the meeting ended without a clear next action, I lost control. |

Log everything in the Assembly Line tracker — the meeting outcome, objections, next steps, and follow-up dates.
