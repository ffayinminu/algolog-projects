## Revenue Is Not Profit -- Why Busy Doesn't Mean Profitable

The most common thing we hear from serviced apartment operators is: "We had a great month -- we were almost fully booked!" But the question that matters is different: were you actually profitable?

High occupancy does not equal high profit. You can be 80% occupied and still losing money if:

- **Payments aren't being collected properly (collection leakage).** Guests confirm bookings, say they'll transfer, and the payment never arrives -- or arrives partially and no one follows up.
- **Staff costs eat into margins during peak periods.** When you're fully booked, you need more cleaning, more reception coverage, more maintenance responses. Those costs scale with occupancy, but revenue only scales if rates hold.
- **You're discounting too aggressively without tracking the impact.** Running a 30% discount to fill rooms feels like a win until you realise the rooms would have filled at 15% off -- or even at full rate with better visibility.
- **Utility and maintenance costs aren't allocated per room.** You know your total electricity bill, but do you know what each room costs you to operate per night? Without that number, you can't price accurately.
- **Guest acquisition cost is higher than you think.** Every new guest costs marketing spend, staff time, platform fees, and onboarding effort. If you're spending NGN 15,000 to acquire a guest who books one night at NGN 25,000, the math doesn't work.

The flip side is equally important: **low occupancy does not equal failure.** A property at 55% occupancy with premium rates, strong payment collection, and a 40% guest return rate can outperform one at 85% occupancy with low rates and payment gaps.

> "The question isn't 'how busy are we?' It's 'how much of what we earn do we actually keep?'"

## The 5 Numbers Every Operator Should Know

These are the five metrics that separate operators who are truly profitable from those who are just busy. Each one takes less than 2 minutes to calculate.

### NUMBER 1: Occupancy Rate

**What it is:** The percentage of your available room-nights that are actually booked and occupied.

**How to calculate it:**
```
Occupancy Rate = (Booked Room-Nights / Total Available Room-Nights) x 100
```

**Example:** If you have 10 rooms and the month has 30 days, you have 300 available room-nights. If 195 were booked, your occupancy rate is 65%.

**Benchmark for a Nigerian 10-unit serviced apartment:**
- 60-75% is healthy
- Below 50%: you likely have a pricing or visibility problem
- Above 80%: you may be undercharging -- or you're in peak season and should be raising rates

---

### NUMBER 2: RevPAR (Revenue Per Available Room-Night)

**What it is:** The single most important metric in hospitality. It combines how full you are with how much you charge, giving you one number that tells you how well your rooms are performing.

**How to calculate it:**
```
RevPAR = Total Room Revenue / Total Available Room-Nights
```
Or simply:
```
RevPAR = Occupancy Rate x Average Nightly Rate
```

**Example:** If your average rate is NGN 30,000 and your occupancy is 65%, your RevPAR is NGN 19,500. This means that across all your rooms -- booked and unbooked -- each room is generating NGN 19,500 per night on average.

**Benchmark:** For a mid-range serviced apartment in Abuja charging NGN 25,000-35,000 per night, a RevPAR of NGN 16,000-22,000 indicates healthy performance.

> "This is the one number that tells you more than any other. It combines how full you are with how much you charge."

---

### NUMBER 3: Payment Collection Rate

**What it is:** The percentage of money you invoiced that you actually received. This is where many Nigerian serviced apartments lose money without realising it.

**How to calculate it:**
```
Collection Rate = (Total Payments Actually Received / Total Payments Invoiced) x 100
```

**Benchmark:**
- 95%+ is where you should be
- Below 90% means you have a serious collection problem that is directly reducing your profit

**Common causes of leakage:**
- Unconfirmed bank transfers ("I'll send it now" -- and then silence)
- Cash payments not recorded by staff
- Partial payments not followed up to completion
- Deposits collected but balance never converted to full payment
- Refunds given without proper documentation

---

### NUMBER 4: Guest Return Rate

**What it is:** The percentage of your guests who have stayed with you before. This is your most powerful profitability indicator because returning guests cost almost nothing to acquire.

**How to calculate it:**
```
Guest Return Rate = (Returning Guests in Period / Total Guests in Period) x 100
```

**Benchmark:**
- 25-40% is good
- Above 40% is excellent
- Below 20%: your guest experience needs attention

**Why it matters:** A returning guest costs you almost nothing to acquire. They already know your property, they've already had a good experience, and they book directly -- no marketing spend, no platform fees, no onboarding time. Every percentage point increase in your return rate directly increases your profit margin.

---

### NUMBER 5: Cost Per Booking

**What it is:** How much it costs you to service a single booking, including all operating costs. This is the number most operators have never calculated -- and it's often the reason properties that look busy aren't actually profitable.

**How to calculate it:**
```
Cost Per Booking = Total Operating Costs / Total Bookings in Period
```

Total operating costs include: staff salaries (proportional), marketing spend, utilities, maintenance, cleaning, platform/payment fees, and consumables.

**Benchmark:**
- Should be less than 30% of your average booking value
- If your average booking is NGN 90,000 (3 nights at NGN 30,000), your cost per booking should be under NGN 27,000

> "If it costs you more to service a booking than you're making from it, you're running a charity, not a business."

## How These Numbers Connect -- The Profitability Matrix

These five numbers don't exist in isolation. They interact, and the combination tells you more than any single metric.

**High occupancy + low collection rate = "You're busy but broke."**
You're filling rooms, but the money isn't making it to your bank account. This is one of the most common traps for Nigerian serviced apartments -- the property looks successful, but the actual cash collected tells a different story.

**Low occupancy + high rate + high return rate = "Premium and sustainable."**
You don't need to be fully booked to be profitable. If you're charging premium rates, collecting efficiently, and guests keep coming back, you have a strong, sustainable business.

**High occupancy + high cost per booking = "Working hard, keeping little."**
You're always busy, your staff is stretched, but at the end of the month there's not much left. Your costs are scaling faster than your revenue.

**Low occupancy + low return rate = "Leaking from both ends."**
Not enough guests are coming in, and the ones who do come aren't coming back. This requires attention on both acquisition and experience.

> "Most operators only look at occupancy. That's like judging a restaurant by how full it is without checking if customers actually paid."

Fill this in on the 1st of every month. It takes 10 minutes. After 3 months, you'll see trends that would have taken years to notice otherwise.

### What to do with the results:

- **Occupancy below 60%?** Focus on visibility and pricing. Are you listed on enough platforms? Is your pricing competitive? Could you run midweek or off-peak promotions?
- **Collection below 90%?** Tighten payment rules immediately. No check-in without confirmed payment. Assign one person to reconcile daily.
- **Return rate below 20%?** Your guest experience needs work. Look at reviews, survey past guests, and identify friction points in the stay.
- **Cost per booking above 35%?** Look for automation opportunities. Where are you spending staff time on things a system could handle?

### An honest note:
This worksheet gives you monthly snapshots. That's genuinely valuable -- most operators don't even have this. But the real power comes from tracking these numbers daily, seeing trends in real time, and getting alerts before problems become losses. A monthly snapshot tells you what happened. Daily tracking helps you change what's happening.
