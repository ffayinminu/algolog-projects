# Fast Commitment Plan
## How to Get Nigerian Businesses to Commit Quickly

*Based on: $100M Offers (Hormozi), Psychology of Selling (Tracy), Sell or Be Sold (Cardone), How to Sell to Nigerians (Akin Alabi), and Algolog's actual deal experience.*

---

## The Problem We're Solving

From the March 2026 closing methodology review: 5 meetings, 0 closed. The bottleneck is Layer 2 → Layer 3 conversion. Leads are interested but not committing. Meetings end with "I'll get back to you" which means goodbye forever.

**Root causes:**
1. Meetings don't end with a decision — they end with "I'll think about it"
2. Too much time between meeting and proposal
3. Proposals sent by email without in-person follow-up
4. No urgency mechanism — client has no reason to commit NOW vs. next month
5. No structured close — the ask for the order isn't happening enough

**The Nigerian context (Akin Alabi):**
6. Trust deficit — Nigerians have been burned by vendors who disappear after payment. Every prospect assumes you might be one of them until you PROVE otherwise.
7. The Nigerian buyer needs to feel like they're winning — "Nigerians would rather pay MORE when they feel they are ripping you off than pay less when they feel you are ripping them off." If the value stack doesn't feel overwhelming, they won't commit.
8. Stories close more than features — captivating stories of transformation hook Nigerian customers in ways that feature lists never will.
9. Patience is required — Nigerian buyers ask many questions, don't read details, want personal explanations. The seller who exercises "superhuman patience" wins.
10. Customer service IS the differentiator — in a market where most businesses give terrible service, being responsive, prepared, and respectful is itself a closing tool.

**The goal:** Compress the timeline from first meeting to signed agreement from weeks/months to days — while building the trust and perceived value that Nigerian buyers specifically require.

## The Fast Commitment Framework

### Principle 1: Close in the Meeting, Not After It

**The rule:** Every meeting should end with one of three outcomes:
1. **Signed agreement** (best case)
2. **Specific date and time for the decision** (acceptable)
3. **Clear "no"** (better than "maybe" — frees you to move on)

"I'll think about it" and "I'll get back to you" are NOT acceptable outcomes.

**How to implement:**

**A. Set the frame at the START:**
> "I appreciate your time. What I'd like to do today is understand your situation, show you how we'd solve it, and at the end, you tell me honestly — does this make sense or not? I'd rather hear a clear no than have us both wondering. Is that fair?"

This gives you permission to push for a decision at the end.

**B. Bring the proposal TO the meeting:**

Don't present the problem, leave, write a proposal, email it, then wait. Instead:
- Diagnose in the first 15 minutes
- Position in the next 10 minutes
- Pull up a proposal template on your laptop/tablet and fill in their specifics LIVE
- Present the investment while you're still sitting with them

> "Based on everything you've just told me, here's what the deployment would look like and what the investment is. Let's go through it together."

**C. Ask for the order in the meeting:**
> "Does this make sense for your business?"
> "Would you like to move forward?"
> "Can we get started?"

If yes → pull up the agreement. Sign now. Send invoice same day.

If hesitation → handle the objection right there (don't defer it to email).

### Principle 2: Eliminate the Gap Between Meeting and Proposal

**The old way (slow):**
Meeting → Go back to office → Write proposal (1-3 days) → Email proposal → Wait for response (days/weeks) → Chase → Maybe get a second meeting → Maybe close

**The new way (fast):**
Meeting → Present investment in the meeting → Close in the meeting → Send agreement + invoice same day

**If you genuinely can't close in the meeting** (e.g., they need to involve a decision-maker who isn't present), the maximum acceptable gap is:

| Action | Timeline |
|--------|----------|
| Proposal/implementation doc sent | Same day as meeting (within 4 hours) |
| Follow-up call to confirm receipt | Next morning |
| Second meeting with decision-maker | Within 3 business days |
| Close | In the second meeting |

**The rule:** Never let a proposal sit for more than 48 hours without a live follow-up. After 48 hours, they've moved on mentally.

---

### Principle 3: Make the Cost of Waiting Visible

People don't act on opportunity. They act on loss. (Tracy: fear of loss is 2.5x stronger than desire for gain.)

**In every meeting, calculate the cost of inaction WITH the client:**

> "You said [manual process] takes [X hours] per week. Over the next 3 months — while you're deciding — that's [Y hours] of staff time. At what rate? That's NGN [Z] spent on something a system would do automatically."

> "You said giving drops 40% during holidays. The next holiday is [date]. If we don't have this live by then, that's another [NGN X] in giving that doesn't happen."

> "You said you're paying Airbnb 15% commission. Over the next 6 months, on your current booking volume, that's NGN [X] going to Airbnb instead of your account."

**Put the math on paper. Show them the number. Leave it in front of them while you present the solution.**

The client isn't choosing between "buy now" and "buy later." They're choosing between "stop the bleeding now" and "keep bleeding for 3 more months while you think about it."

---

### Principle 4: Remove Every Friction Point from Saying Yes

Every obstacle between "I want this" and "I've paid for this" is a chance for them to change their mind.

**Friction killers:**

| Friction | How to Remove It |
|----------|-----------------|
| "I need to see a proposal first" | Bring the proposal to the meeting. Present it live. |
| "I need to discuss with my partner" | Get the partner on a call NOW, or schedule for tomorrow. Don't let it drift. |
| "I need to review the agreement" | Walk through the agreement together in the meeting. Answer questions in real time. |
| "I'll transfer the payment this week" | Send the invoice while still in the meeting. "I've just sent the invoice to your email — you can do the transfer whenever is convenient today." |
| "I need approval from my board" | Offer to join the board meeting or do a 15-minute Zoom with the decision-maker. |
| "I want to see it working first" | Demo on your phone RIGHT NOW. Show a live Spacer property. |
| "What if it doesn't work?" | State the guarantee: "If it's not working within 30 days, we keep working until it does. No additional cost." |
| "Can you give me time to think?" | "Of course. What specifically do you want to think about? Maybe I can help clarify right now." |

### Principle 5: Create Real Urgency (Not Fake Scarcity)

**Urgency mechanisms that are genuine:**

#### A. The Cost of Delay Calculator
Use in every meeting. Make it specific to their business:

| Industry | The Cost Calculation |
|----------|---------------------|
| Hospitality | "You lose NGN [15% × avg booking × monthly volume] to Airbnb commissions every month. Each month without your own portal = that amount gone." |
| Oil & Gas | "You process [X] vouchers daily on paper. One disputed balance costs [NGN Y]. How many months of risk can you afford?" |
| Churches | "Giving drops [X]% during holidays. Next holiday is [date]. Without automated giving, that's [NGN Y] lost again." |
| Retail | "You serve [X] customers monthly with zero repeat mechanism. At [Y]% potential return rate × [NGN Z] average basket = [NGN W] monthly revenue sitting uncaptured." |

#### B. Seasonal Windows
For industries with natural cycles:
- **Hotels:** "Peak season is [month]. Deploy now and capture it digitally. Wait, and it's another peak on WhatsApp."
- **Churches:** "Easter/Christmas is [X] weeks away. Pledged needs [Y] weeks to set up + member adoption time."
- **Schools:** "New term starts [date]. Fee collection is the biggest pain. Deploy before term starts or wait another full term."
- **BDCs:** "Quarter-end reconciliation is [date]. Automated or manual — your choice."

#### C. Implementation Queue
When genuinely true:
> "We're currently deploying for [X] clients. If we start this month, you're live by [date]. If it pushes to next month, the timeline extends to [later date] because of our current commitments."

#### D. Early Start Momentum
> "We can start configuration now while you finalize payment. That way when the transfer lands, we're already halfway through setup. You're not losing days."

Once work has started — even just configuration — the psychological commitment is made. It's much harder to back out of something already in motion.

### Principle 6: The 72-Hour Close Protocol

For every warm lead, apply this 72-hour protocol from first meeting to close:

#### Hour 0: The Meeting
- Diagnose → Position → Handle Objections → Close
- Present the investment IN the meeting
- Ask for the order at least 3 times
- If not closed in meeting, get a SPECIFIC next step with a date

#### Hour 0-4: Same Day Follow-Up
- Send the agreement/implementation doc (if not signed in meeting)
- Send a WhatsApp: "Great meeting today. I've sent the [document]. Let me know if any questions come up."
- Do NOT send a long recap email. They were there. Keep it short.

#### Hour 24: Day 2 Follow-Up
- Call (not text): "I wanted to make sure you received the document. Did anything stand out that we should discuss?"
- If they raise an objection → handle it on this call (Agree → Redirect → Close)
- If they say they haven't read it → "No problem. Would it help if we went through it together for 10 minutes right now?"

#### Hour 48: Day 3 — The Decision Push
- If still no commitment: "I want to respect your time, so let me be straightforward. Based on our conversation, this solves [hot button]. The cost of waiting is [cost of inaction number]. Is there something specific holding you back that I can address?"
- If they name an objection → handle it
- If they say "I'm just busy" → "I understand. When is the best time for me to call you tomorrow so we can finalize? I want to make sure this doesn't fall through the cracks for either of us."

#### Hour 72: Day 4 — Close or Schedule
- If still not committed → schedule a face-to-face meeting (not another call) within the next 2 days
- "I think it would be helpful to sit down one more time — I may not have explained [aspect] clearly enough. Can I come to your office Thursday?"
- Tracy: chance of sale increases 10-20x face-to-face

#### Beyond 72 Hours: The Long Follow-Up
If they haven't committed after the 72-hour protocol:
- Move to weekly follow-up (Cardone: follow up for years if needed)
- Vary the medium: call → WhatsApp → email → personal visit → voice note
- Never bring up unreturned calls or messages (Cardone)
- Never make them wrong for the delay
- Add value in every touch: share a relevant article, a case study, an industry insight — don't just say "checking in"

### Principle 7: Stack the Offer So the Price Feels Small

When you present the investment, the client should already feel like they're getting 5-10x the value. Stack everything:

**Example (Spacer for a 20-unit short-let property at NGN 5,000,000):**

> "Here's what's included in the deployment:"
>
> ✅ Branded booking portal with your property name, photos, and pricing
> ✅ Online payment processing — guests pay before they arrive
> ✅ Smart lock integration — access codes generated automatically, expire at checkout
> ✅ Auto-generated property marketing website
> ✅ Cart abandonment recovery — automatic reminders for incomplete bookings
> ✅ Real-time occupancy dashboard — see every room's status at a glance
> ✅ Invoice and receipt generation for every booking
> ✅ Staff role management — receptionist, manager, cashier access levels
> ✅ Conflict prevention — no more double bookings
> ✅ Automated notifications — booking confirmations, checkout reminders, payment receipts
> ✅ Full team training + user manual
> ✅ 30-day operations guarantee
> ✅ Ongoing support within scope — no maintenance fees
>
> "The total investment is NGN 5,000,000."
>
> "Now, consider: Airbnb charges 15% on every booking. At your volume, that's NGN 1.08M/year. This system eliminates that. Cart abandonment recovery typically recaptures 15-25% of abandoned bookings. For you, that could be another NGN 500K-1M/year in revenue that would have been lost. And the hours your team spends on WhatsApp coordination, manual confirmations, and key handovers — what's that worth per month?"
>
> "You're not spending NGN 5,000,000. You're spending NGN 5,000,000 to save and recover NGN 2-3,000,000 per year. By year two, the system has paid for itself and everything after that is pure gain."

### Principle 8: Use the Deposit to Lock Commitment

Once someone says "yes" verbally, get financial commitment as fast as possible. Every minute between "yes" and payment is a minute where doubt can creep in.

**The protocol:**

1. They say yes → "Great. I'll send the agreement and invoice right now."
2. Send WHILE STILL IN THE MEETING (from your phone if needed)
3. "Can I confirm your email? I've just sent it."
4. "There's no rush on the full amount — but a deposit locks your spot in our implementation queue. Can you do the transfer today or tomorrow?"
5. If they can't do full upfront: "Would it work to do [smaller amount] now to get us started, and the balance before we go live?"

**Why this works:**
- Cardone: "The worst part of making a sale is you just lost your best prospect." Lock the commitment immediately.
- Tracy: People buy emotionally and justify logically. The emotion is highest RIGHT AFTER they say yes. Capture it before it cools.
- A deposit, even a small one, creates psychological commitment. Once they've paid something, backing out feels like losing money.

---

### Principle 9: The "Start Now, Pay Later" Bridge

For clients who are sold but genuinely have cash flow timing issues:

> "I understand the timing. Here's what I'd suggest — let's start the setup and configuration this week. The first [2-3 weeks] is all planning, content gathering, and system configuration. By the time we need to move into active development, the payment can be in place. This way you don't lose [X weeks] just waiting for the budget cycle."

**Why this works:**
- Work has started = commitment is made
- They feel the momentum — it's harder to cancel something in progress than something that hasn't started
- You're demonstrating trust: "We're willing to start because we're confident you'll follow through"
- The alternative (waiting until budget is ready) means they cool off and the deal dies

**Guardrail:** Only use this with clients who have shown strong intent and where you have reason to believe payment will come. Don't do this for "I'll think about it" clients.

---

### Principle 10: Create a Commitment Pathway, Not a Cliff

Many deals die because the ask feels too big. "Sign this agreement for NGN 10,000,000" is a cliff. Build a pathway instead.

**The pathway:**

| Step | What You Ask For | Feels Like |
|------|-----------------|-----------|
| 1 | "Can I get 15 minutes to understand your business?" | Almost nothing |
| 2 | "Can I show you how we'd solve this?" (same meeting) | They're already here |
| 3 | "Does this make sense for your business?" (trial close) | Just an opinion |
| 4 | "Would you like to see the investment?" | Curiosity, not commitment |
| 5 | "Based on this, can we move forward?" | Natural next step |
| 6 | "I'll send the agreement now — shall I use this email?" | Administrative detail |
| 7 | "The deposit locks your implementation slot" | Small action, big commitment |

Each step is small and feels natural. By step 7, they've said "yes" six times already. The final yes is just one more in a series.

**Tracy's analogy:** "Like digits in a phone number — you must dial each in sequence."

## The Nigerian Commitment Accelerators (Akin Alabi)

These are specific to selling in Nigeria and should be layered on top of the universal principles above.

### A. Build Trust BEFORE You Sell

Nigerian buyers don't trust easily — and they shouldn't. They've been burned. Your job is to prove you're different before you ever mention price.

**Trust builders specific to Nigeria:**

| Action | Why It Works |
|--------|-------------|
| **Meet at YOUR office** | Having a physical office in Abuja that they can visit proves you're real. Many Nigerian tech vendors operate from WhatsApp and disappear. An office = permanence. |
| **Show a LIVE system on your phone** | Not a mockup, not a PDF, not a PowerPoint. Pull up a real Spacer property and let them see bookings happening in real time. Alabi: "People believe what they see, not what they hear." |
| **Name your existing clients** (with permission) | "We deployed this at Qas Luxury Apartments in Abuja. We deployed at Alegre Farms in Bwari." Named clients with verifiable locations build instant credibility. |
| **Offer an office visit to a deployed property** | "Would you like to visit one of our properties and see it running live?" This offer alone builds trust — even if they don't take you up on it. The willingness to show is proof. |
| **Dress professionally** | In Nigeria, appearance matters enormously. Alabi + Tracy + Cardone all agree: dress like the most successful person in the room. Nigerian business owners judge competence by presentation. |
| **Printed materials** | Bring a physical proposal, printed company profile, printed one-pager. In Nigeria, digital-only feels temporary. Something they can hold feels real. |
| **Follow through on small promises** | If you say "I'll send the proposal by 4pm" — send it by 3pm. Every kept promise builds the trust account. Nigerian buyers are watching whether you do what you say before they commit money. |

### B. Make the Offer Feel Like a Steal

Alabi's core insight: **Nigerians want to feel like they're winning the deal.** Not that you're giving them a discount — that you're giving them so much value that the price feels like robbery (in their favour).

**How to apply:**

1. **Stack the value visually.** List everything included. Make the list LONG. When they see 15 items included for one price, the price feels small. When they see 3 items, they calculate per-item and feel ripped off.

2. **Never show individual line-item costs.** Show the bundled total with "Included" next to every item. This makes them feel like they're getting extras for free. If you show "Dashboard: NGN 500,000, Portal: NGN 300,000" — they'll negotiate each line. If you show "Dashboard: Included, Portal: Included, Total: NGN X" — they feel like the dashboard and portal are bonuses.

3. **Anchor against the cost of NOT buying.** Before you state your price, state the cost of their current problem. "You're losing NGN 1.08M/year to Airbnb commissions. You're spending NGN 250,000/month on reconciliation staff time. You've had 3 disputed balances this year at NGN 500,000 each." THEN state your price. It feels tiny against the loss.

4. **Use the "second money" principle.** Once they say yes to the main system, add bonuses: "And because we're doing the full deployment, I'll include the staff training and the user manual at no extra charge. And the first 30 days of support are included." They feel like they're getting more and more. Cardone: "Second money is easier than first money."

### C. Tell Stories, Not Features

Alabi: "Captivating stories hook potential customers." In Nigeria especially, a story of someone like them who solved the same problem carries 10x the weight of a feature list.

**The story formula for Nigerian buyers:**

1. "A [business type] in [Nigerian city] was doing [manual process]..."
2. "They were dealing with [specific pain — use emotional language]..."
3. "We built [solution] for them..."
4. "Within [timeframe], [specific measurable result]..."
5. "They told us: '[quote that captures the emotional transformation]'"

**Have 3-4 stories ready at all times:**
- Saadatu's Apartment (Kaduna) — for hospitality prospects
- Starbuck Oil & Gas (Abuja) — for oil & gas prospects
- Church deployment — for religious organisation prospects
- Court 474 — for property management prospects

### D. Exercise Superhuman Patience

Alabi: "A lot of Nigerians are impatient and do not pay attention to details. The trick is to exercise superhuman patience when dealing with Nigerian customers and tolerate questions by taking time to explain."

**What this means in practice:**

- They will ask the same question 3 different ways. Answer all 3 without showing frustration.
- They will not read your proposal before the meeting. Be prepared to walk through it verbally.
- They will bring up concerns you already addressed. Address them again, calmly, as if for the first time.
- They will want to negotiate even when the price is fair. Use the Agree → Redirect → Close method.
- They may show up late to meetings, reschedule last-minute, or ghost for days then reappear. None of this means "no." It means they're Nigerian and busy. Follow up without making them wrong.

**The patience advantage:** Most of your competitors lose their patience at step 3. If you're still there at step 7, calm and helpful, you win by default. Alabi: "If you treat a customer right like a king or a queen, you already won their heart and their money."

### E. Don't Sell to Friends and Family

Alabi specifically warns against relying on personal networks: "Few things ruin relationships more than money." This is important for Algolog because Faridah's leads are personal contacts.

**The rule:** Treat warm leads with the SAME professionalism as cold leads. Don't assume the friendship means the sale is easy. If anything, personal contacts require MORE formality — printed proposals, clear terms, written agreements — because informality breeds misunderstandings that destroy both the deal and the relationship.

### F. Service After the Sale = The Next Sale

In Nigeria, the relationship doesn't end at payment — it begins. How you deliver, how you support, how you respond to issues AFTER the sale determines:
- Whether they refer you to others (word-of-mouth is king in Nigeria)
- Whether they buy the next product from you
- Whether they become a testimonial you can use

**Post-sale commitment:**
- Check in within 48 hours of deployment: "How is everything running?"
- Check in at 1 week: "Any questions from your team?"
- Check in at 1 month: "Here's a summary of your first month's data. How do you feel about it?"
- Each check-in is also a chance to ask: "Do you know anyone else who could benefit from this?"

Alabi: "Customer service is at the heart of every business."

## The One Rule

> **If you leave a meeting without asking for the order, you didn't have a meeting — you had a conversation.**

Every meeting is a closing opportunity. Every conversation is a chance to advance toward commitment. The client is waiting for you to lead. If you don't ask, someone else will.

In Nigeria specifically: the client may WANT to buy but is waiting for you to make it easy. They need to feel the value is overwhelming, the trust is established, and the risk is zero. Stack the value. Show the proof. State the guarantee. Then ask for the order. And ask again.
